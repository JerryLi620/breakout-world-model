You're right to push on architecture. Let me name the actual problem before swapping anything.

Teacher-forced predictions shouldn't decay — each step uses real input frames, not its own outputs. So "ball/paddle fade after a few teacher-forced frames" doesn't mean the model is rolling out into a static basin. It means the model is underfitting one-step prediction itself, and what looks like "later frames are worse" is just the model is bad at every step independently and you happen to look at later ones.

The structural reason it can't fit one batch crisply:

Encoder bottlenecks 4×64×64 → 256-dim global latent → decoder upsamples 4→8→16→32→64.

A 256-dim global vector has to encode "ball is at pixel (47, 32) with sub-pixel offset, paddle is at column 28, all 50 bricks intact." Then the decoder has to draw a 1–2 pixel ball at a precise location after upsampling from a 4×4 feature map. That bottleneck cannot represent crisp 1-pixel ball position, even with overfit. So you get a blurry low-intensity blob that fades into background — exactly what you see.

The GRU and the decoder type are not the bug. The bottleneck-without-skips is. Two architectural fixes that actually work for Breakout-style next-frame prediction:

Option A — U-Net with skip connections (recommended). Keep encoder + GRU + decoder structure, but route encoder feature maps directly to the matching decoder layers. The skips bypass the 4×4 bottleneck and carry pixel-level detail. The global latent + GRU still models dynamics (where the ball will go); the skips give the decoder the substrate to draw it crisply.

Option B — purely convolutional residual predictor (simpler, even more reliable for one-step). Drop the global latent and GRU entirely for the one-step head. Stack convs over the 4-frame input + action-broadcast channel, output a 64×64 delta. No bottleneck. For overfitting one batch this will work in <500 steps. Keep the latent/GRU branch only for autoregressive rollout later.

Both are real fixes, not band-aids. Option A is closer to your current code and keeps the world-model framing (useful for rollout later). Option B is the fastest way to prove the dataset + loss are sane and give you a clean overfit_teacher_forced_pred.gif today.

Want me to implement A or B? I'd recommend A — it solves the immediate crisp-prediction problem and leaves the rollout/dynamics path in place so step-2 of your plan still works.

Good question, and the honest answer is: you basically are trying to imitate them, but the SOTA papers hide a lot of compute and engineering behind their architecture diagrams. Let me give you the real picture so you can decide what to copy.

What SOTA Atari world models actually use

Model Core trick Frames trained on Compute
World Models (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018) VAE encoder → MDN-RNN dynamics → decoder ~10k rollouts 1 GPU-day
PlaNet / Dreamer / DreamerV2 / V3 (Hafner et al.) RSSM: deterministic GRU + stochastic latent (categorical or Gaussian) 1M–200M env steps days on 1 GPU
IRIS / TWM (2022–23) Discrete VQ-VAE tokenizer + Transformer over token sequence 1M+ frames 1–4 GPU-days
DIAMOND (2024) Encoder → diffusion model as the next-frame predictor 100M frames many GPU-days
Genie / GameNGen (2024) Big spatial transformer / diffusion, conditioned on action huge tens to hundreds of GPU-days

Notice three things they all share that your current code does not have:

  1. They never bottleneck pixel detail through a 256-dim vector. Dreamer keeps the latent (~1024-dim categorical) for dynamics, but the decoder is convolutional with the spatial structure preserved by the design of the latent. IRIS/Genie/DIAMOND push spatial detail through a token grid (e.g., 8×8 or 16×16 tokens, each with rich content) or a full spatial diffusion prior — not a single flat vector.
  2. They train at scale. "Overfitting one batch" is not a regime any of these papers benchmarks — they assume tens of thousands to millions of frames. The reason your one-batch overfit is hard isn't that they have a magic loss; it's that they have so much data that the model never has to memorize a single batch crisply, just predict on average.
  3. They lean on either (a) a stochastic latent or (b) a sharp generative decoder. Pixel L1/L2 alone produces blur — that's a 50-year-old problem. Dreamer adds a KL term on a stochastic latent. IRIS uses cross-entropy over discrete tokens (no blur — each token is a categorical pick). DIAMOND uses diffusion (sharpens via iterative denoising). None of them rely on plain weighted L1 to get crisp output.

Why "just copy" is harder than it looks

  • RSSM (Dreamer-style) is the smallest sensible thing to copy. But the latent is stochastic and trained with KL balancing, free bits, and a specific KL schedule. People who reimplement Dreamer without those tricks usually get blurry predictions. The official codebase is ~3,000 lines of careful JAX/TF.
  • IRIS-style tokenizer + Transformer is closer to a clean win because the discrete tokens kill the blur problem outright. But you need to train a VQ-VAE first, then train a transformer dynamics model on token sequences. Two-stage, ~1000 lines, and the VQ-VAE itself has known instability issues (codebook collapse).
  • Diffusion / DIAMOND gives the best visual quality but is the most expensive — you're training a denoiser conditioned on past frames and actions, and you do many forward passes per generated frame.

The pragmatic ranking for your situation

If your goal is "overfit one batch of Breakout and see a crisp moving ball today":

  1. U-Net with skips + conv residual head (closest to what you have). Will overfit one batch crisply within an hour. Won't generalize as a world model, but solves the one-step debugging problem cleanly. This is what I recommended last turn.
  2. Tiny RSSM (Dreamer-lite): convolutional encoder + stochastic latent + decoder with KL. ~500 extra lines. Real path to a working rollout. About a weekend of work to get right.
  3. VQ-VAE + small transformer (IRIS-lite): best one-step image quality, hardest to debug; codebook collapse is real. Probably 1–2 weeks.
  4. Diffusion world model: not realistic for one person on a laptop in a class project. Skip.

Clear goal. Let me re-frame what you actually need and what's realistic.

What "stochastic rollout" really means — two flavors

  1. Action-conditioned: same initial frames + different action sequence → different futures. The dynamics themselves are deterministic given the action. Easy. Your current architecture is already shaped for this if it worked.
  2. Truly stochastic dynamics: same initial frames + same actions → still different futures, because the model samples from a learned distribution over next-states at every step. This is what Dreamer / IRIS / DIAMOND do. Hard.

You almost certainly want flavor 2 (otherwise on a fixed action sequence, the model is just trying to predict one true future). That requires three things your current code does not have:

Requirement Your code today What's needed
Spatial detail in decoder 4×4 bottleneck, no skips → blur U-Net skips, or discrete token grid, or diffusion
Stochastic next-state noise_std is just additive Gaussian on z — not learned Learned p(z_{t+1} | z_t, a_t), sample at rollout time
Train signal for distribution L1 only → predicts the mean of all futures → blur KL term (Dreamer) or cross-entropy on tokens (IRIS)

The fact that L1 predicts the mean of possible futures is the deep reason your predictions blur and fade. Even with a perfect U-Net, if the ball can go to (47,32) or (47,33) next, L1 will draw it at half-intensity at both places. That's why stochastic latents matter — they break the mean.

The three realistic architectures (ranked for your situation)

Dreamer V3-style RSSM (recommended) - Conv encoder → deterministic GRU + categorical stochastic latent z_t (e.g., 32 categories × 32 dims, one-hot) → conv decoder. - Loss = pixel reconstruction + KL(posterior ‖ prior) on the categorical, with KL-balancing. - At rollout, sample z_{t+1} from prior categorical → real stochasticity. - ~600 lines on top of what you have. Most copied open implementation: danijar/dreamerv3. - Caveat: pixel sharpness on 64×64 grayscale Atari is OK-not-great. Good enough to see a moving ball with stochastic variation.

IRIS-style (VQ-VAE tokenizer + small transformer) - Stage 1: train VQ-VAE on frames → each frame becomes e.g. an 8×8 grid of discrete tokens. - Stage 2: train a small transformer to predict next-frame tokens given past tokens + action. Sample with temperature. - Crisper images than Dreamer. Real stochasticity via token sampling. But two-stage training, codebook collapse is a real failure mode. - ~1500 lines, ~1–2 weeks if you've done it before, 2–4 if you haven't.

Conditional video diffusion (DIAMOND) - Best visual quality. Not realistic on a laptop / class-project budget.
