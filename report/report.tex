%%%%%%%%% ABSTRACT
\begin{abstract}
We study \emph{action-conditioned world models} that learn to simulate Atari
Breakout: given a few starting frames and a sequence of actions, the model
should generate a coherent, game-like continuation. We first build a
deterministic next-frame predictor---a convolutional U-Net with
feature-wise linear modulation (FiLM) for action conditioning and a bounded
residual decoder---and show that, while it learns clean one-step predictions,
it cannot produce stable autoregressive rollouts. We characterize this failure
precisely: under closed-loop generation the model's error compounds, and the
\emph{type} of collapse (brightness blow-up vs.\ motion freeze) is governed by a single regularization weight alone, with no stable setting in between.
This is the classic exposure-bias wall of pixel-regression models. Motivated by
this analysis, we switch to a two-stage discrete-token model in the style of
recent transformer world models: a VQ-VAE tokenizer encodes each
$128\times128$ frame into a $16\times16$ grid of discrete codes, and a GPT-style
causal transformer models the next-frame tokens conditioned on past tokens and
actions, trained with cross-entropy and generated by temperature sampling. The
token model produces coherent rollouts over the full clip with no blow-up or
freeze, and---because generation is stochastic---different samples from the same
prime yield different plausible futures. We further diagnose a ball-respawn
limitation as a \emph{data-coverage} problem rather than a modeling failure and
fix it at the data-collection level. These results confirm our hypothesis:
L1 pixel regression hits a fundamental rollout wall, while discrete tokenization
plus autoregressive sampling breaks it.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

A \emph{world model} learns the dynamics of an environment: given the recent
history of observations and an action, it predicts the next observation. If the
predictions are accurate and stable, the model can be unrolled to ``imagine''
entire trajectories, which is useful for planning, model-based reinforcement
learning, and as a learned simulator~\cite{ha2018worldmodels,hafner2020dreamer}.
We study this problem on Atari Breakout, where the dynamics---a ball bouncing off
a paddle and walls, breaking bricks---are simple to describe but surprisingly
hard to generate frame-by-frame, because the ball is only one or two pixels wide
at the resolutions practical for training.

Our goal is concrete: prime the model with a few real frames plus a sequence of
actions, and roll out a clip that ``looks like someone is playing the game.''
The central difficulty is not one-step prediction but \emph{autoregressive
rollout}: when the model consumes its own predictions, small errors compound.

We make two contributions. First, we build a strong deterministic baseline (a
FiLM-conditioned U-Net with a bounded residual decoder) and show through
controlled experiments that it learns clean one-step predictions yet cannot roll
out stably. We characterize the failure as a trade-off between two collapse
modes governed by a single brightness-regularization weight, with no stable
operating point, and argue this is the exposure-bias wall inherent to pixel
regression. Second, motivated by this analysis, we implement a discrete-token
world model (VQ-VAE tokenizer + causal transformer) and show it produces
coherent, \emph{stochastic}, action-conditioned rollouts that the deterministic
model cannot achieve. We also present a failure analysis (ball respawn) and a
data-level fix. The result is both a working model and a clear story about
\emph{why} the simpler approach fails.

\section{Related Work}

\textbf{Latent world models for control.} Ha and
Schmidhuber~\cite{ha2018worldmodels} introduced the
encoder--dynamics--decoder template that we follow: an observation is compressed
to a latent, a recurrent network rolls the latent forward under actions, and a
decoder maps back to pixels. The PlaNet/Dreamer
line~\cite{hafner2019planet,hafner2020dreamer,hafner2021dreamerv2,hafner2023dreamerv3}
develops this into the Recurrent State-Space Model (RSSM), which couples a
deterministic recurrent path with a \emph{stochastic} latent trained by a
variational (KL) objective, and learns behaviors purely ``in imagination''
inside the model. A pivotal step for our purposes is
DreamerV2~\cite{hafner2021dreamerv2}, which replaced the Gaussian latent with a
\emph{discrete} (categorical) one and reported that this change---not a larger
network---was decisive for mastering Atari; DreamerV3~\cite{hafner2023dreamerv3}
later scaled the same recipe across many domains. This RSSM family is the
natural third point of comparison to the two models we study: it is stochastic
like our token model but keeps a \emph{continuous} reconstruction decoder like
our deterministic baseline. We do not implement it here for budget reasons, but
its trajectory---from continuous to discrete latents---directly motivates our
question of \emph{why} discreteness helps at rollout time.

\textbf{Pixel-space video prediction and the averaging problem.}
SimPLe~\cite{kaiser2020simple} learns an action-conditioned, pixel-space
video-prediction model of Atari and uses it to train a policy; architecturally
it is the closest prior to our deterministic baseline, and like ours it
regresses future frames rather than sampling them. The known failure of such
regressors is that a squared- or absolute-error objective is minimized by the
\emph{conditional mean} of the future, so when several futures are plausible the
network outputs their blurry average~\cite{mathieu2016deep}. Stochastic
video-prediction models such as SV2P~\cite{babaeizadeh2018stochastic} address
this by injecting latent variables so that distinct futures can be sampled
rather than averaged. Our deterministic model inherits exactly the mean-seeking
pathology these works describe; the blow-up and freeze regimes we characterize
(\S\ref{sec:det}) are two ways that pathology manifests under closed-loop
rollout.

\textbf{Exposure bias and compounding rollout error.} The second ingredient in
our baseline's failure is \emph{exposure bias}: a model trained only on
ground-truth context (teacher forcing) is never exposed to its own imperfect
predictions, so at test time small errors push the input off the training
distribution and compound~\cite{bengio2015scheduled,ranzato2016sequence}.
Scheduled sampling~\cite{bengio2015scheduled} and sequence-level
training~\cite{ranzato2016sequence} were proposed in language and sequence
modeling to mitigate this; we observe the same distribution-shift mechanism in
the visual domain, where it appears as monotonic brightness drift or motion
collapse rather than text degeneration.

\textbf{Token / generative world models.} A complementary remedy replaces
regression with \emph{classification over discrete codes}. VQ-VAE~\cite{oord2017vqvae}
learns a discrete codebook for images; Image GPT~\cite{chen2020igpt} and
VideoGPT~\cite{yan2021videogpt} model the resulting tokens autoregressively, and
VQGAN~\cite{esser2021taming} adds an adversarial decoder for sharp
high-resolution synthesis. IRIS~\cite{micheli2023iris} brings this recipe to
model-based RL (a VQ-VAE tokenizer plus a transformer dynamics model over
tokens and actions) and is the direct template for our token model;
TWM~\cite{robine2023twm} and STORM~\cite{zhang2023storm} are closely related
transformer world models that also report strong sample efficiency on Atari.
At the high-fidelity end, DIAMOND~\cite{alonso2024diamond},
GameNGen~\cite{valevski2024gamengen}, and Genie~\cite{bruce2024genie} use
diffusion or large transformers to build playable world models; these are far
beyond our compute budget but share the core lesson that a strong generative
decoder avoids the blur and decay of pixel regression.

\textbf{Components and positioning.} Our deterministic model uses a
U-Net~\cite{ronneberger2015unet} for spatial detail and FiLM~\cite{perez2018film}
for action conditioning, and the token model uses the standard causal
transformer~\cite{vaswani2017attention}. We claim no architectural novelty: our
token model is a deliberately minimal reimplementation of the IRIS
recipe~\cite{micheli2023iris}, and the broad conclusion that discrete latents
help on Atari was already established by DreamerV2~\cite{hafner2021dreamerv2} and
IRIS. What we contribute, as a course project, is a \emph{controlled,
single-pipeline comparison} that those papers do not report: holding the data,
environment, and evaluation fixed, we show \emph{why} the deterministic baseline
fails by characterizing its failure as a regularization-controlled
blow-up$\leftrightarrow$freeze trade-off (\S\ref{sec:det}), and we measure the
token model on the \emph{same} rollout axes to confirm it stays on ground truth
where the baseline cannot. We treat this as a pedagogical, reproducible
demonstration rather than a new method.

\section{Data}

\textbf{Environment and action space.} We collect trajectories from Atari
Breakout via the Arcade Learning Environment~\cite{bellemare2013ale}
(\texttt{ALE/Breakout-v5}). Breakout exposes four discrete actions
(\texttt{NOOP}, \texttt{FIRE}, \texttt{RIGHT}, \texttt{LEFT}), which we record
per step and one-hot encode as the action input to both models. The dynamics are
deceptively hard to render: the ball is only one to two pixels wide at the
resolutions we can afford, so a model that is even slightly conservative will
let it fade, while the bricks and paddle are large, static, high-contrast
regions that dominate any pixel-averaged loss. This mismatch---a tiny, fast,
information-rich object against a large, slow background---is precisely what
makes the rollout problem interesting on this game.

\textbf{Preprocessing.} Each frame is converted to grayscale and resized to
$128\times128$ with nearest-neighbor sampling, then scaled to $[0,1]$. We use
grayscale because Breakout's dynamics are fully observable in luminance and it
cuts tokenizer and transformer cost threefold; we use nearest-neighbor rather
than bilinear resizing specifically to avoid anti-aliasing the ball into a
sub-threshold gray smear. We initially trained at $64\times64$ (used by the
deterministic baseline, Table~\ref{tab:hp}a) and raised the resolution to
$128\times128$ for the token model to sharpen the ball; the tokenizer is fully
convolutional, so this change requires no architectural modification. An
observation \emph{state} is a stack of the last four frames, which supplies the
velocity information a single frame lacks.

\textbf{Collection policy.} Trajectories are gathered with a random policy and a
frame skip of $2$, which increases per-step visual change and decorrelates
successive frames. Each episode begins with a \texttt{FIRE} action to launch the
ball, and we discard near-static clips whose maximum inter-frame difference falls
below a threshold, since clips in which nothing moves provide no dynamics
signal. We use two corpora collected this way: a length-$20$ set
($2{,}000\times20=40{,}000$ frames) for the main comparison, and a longer
length-$50$ set ($2{,}000\times50=100{,}000$ frames) used for the respawn study
below. The tokenizer trains per frame and the dynamics model on sliding windows
of frames, so even the shorter corpus yields ample supervision. Because the
collection policy is fixed and seeded, the corpora are exactly reproducible; we
report all rollout metrics on a fixed set of $30$ evaluation sequences primed
from this distribution. We note as a limitation that, given the project scope,
we do not carve out a separately held-out test environment seed, a
natural extension for a fuller study.

\textbf{Targeted respawn coverage.} Breakout's life-loss mechanic requires the
agent to press \texttt{FIRE} to launch a new ball after one is lost. With a pure
random policy and short clips, a full
``ball lost $\rightarrow$ \texttt{FIRE} $\rightarrow$ respawn'' cycle almost
never appears in a window, so a world model trained on such data has no chance of
learning it and instead predicts that a lost ball stays gone. This is a
\emph{data-coverage} gap, not a modeling failure, and we fix it at the source: we
monitor the environment's life counter and, whenever a life is lost, force the
next action to be \texttt{FIRE}, combined with the longer length-$50$ clips so a
full cycle fits inside a training window. With this policy, $9$ of $10$ collected
sequences contain a complete respawn cycle, giving the dynamics model the
supervision it needs to reproduce the mechanic faithfully. We highlight this as a
small but general lesson: for rare but mechanically important events, shaping the
\emph{data distribution} can be more effective than adding model capacity.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Overview and problem statement}
Let $x_t\in[0,1]^{H\times W}$ be the grayscale frame at step $t$ and
$a_t\in\{0,\dots,3\}$ the action taken there. We define the model state as the
stack of the last four frames, $s_t=(x_{t-3},x_{t-2},x_{t-1},x_t)$, and seek a
model of the action-conditioned transition $p(x_{t+1}\mid s_t,a_t)$. Both models
we study share this framing---``predict the next frame from a 4-frame stack and
an action''---but differ fundamentally in \emph{representation} and
\emph{objective} (Figure~\ref{fig:arch}). The deterministic model learns a point
estimate $\hat{x}_{t+1}=f_\theta(s_t,a_t)$ by regression; the token model learns
a full distribution over a discrete code of $x_{t+1}$ and samples from it. The
quantity we ultimately care about is not one-step accuracy but the quality of an
\emph{autoregressive rollout}, in which the model is primed with a few real
frames and then consumes its own predictions:
$\hat{x}_{t+1}=f_\theta(\hat{s}_t,a_t)$ with $\hat{s}_t$ built from previous
outputs. This closed loop is where teacher-forced training and test-time use
diverge, and where the two representations behave very differently.

\subsection{Deterministic FiLM U-Net}
A convolutional encoder maps the 4-frame stack to a latent $z_t$ and a set of
spatial skip features. A GRU advances the latent conditioned on the action,
$z_{t+1}=\mathrm{GRU}([z_t, e(a_t)])$, and a U-Net decoder with skip connections
produces the output. We found two design choices essential. (i)~\textbf{Skip
connections}~\cite{ronneberger2015unet}: a flat 256-d latent cannot encode a
1-pixel ball location, so a pure bottleneck produces a blurry, fading ball; skips
route pixel detail to the decoder. (ii)~\textbf{FiLM action conditioning}~\cite{perez2018film}:
concatenating a 4-d action to a 256-d latent makes the action negligible and the
model ignores it; instead we modulate every decoder feature map with per-channel
scale and bias predicted from the action, $h \!\leftarrow\! (1+\gamma(a))\,h +
\beta(a)$, initialized to identity. The decoder predicts a bounded residual,
$\hat{x}_{t+1}=\mathrm{clip}(x_t + s\cdot\tanh(\delta),0,1)$, which keeps
predictions anchored to the previous frame.

\textbf{Objective.} A plain pixel loss is dominated by the static background and
ignores the ball, so we train with a motion-aware loss built from four terms.
Let $x^\star=x_{t+1}$ be the target, $x_t$ the previous frame, and define a
per-pixel weight $w$ that up-weights pixels that either change between $x_t$ and
$x^\star$ or are bright (the ball and paddle). The total loss is
\begin{align}
\mathcal{L} =\;& \mathbb{E}\big[w \cdot \mathrm{SmoothL1}(\hat{x},x^\star)\big]
  + \lambda_1\,\mathcal{L}_{\Delta} \nonumber\\
  &+ \lambda_2\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{false-mot}}
  + \lambda_3\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{bright}} ,
\end{align}
where (i) the weighted $\mathrm{SmoothL1}$ term is the reconstruction loss; (ii)
$\mathcal{L}_{\Delta}=\mathrm{SmoothL1}(\hat{x}-x_t,\;x^\star-x_t)$ supervises the
\emph{change} from the previous frame directly, forcing the model to predict motion
rather than copy $x_t$; (iii)
$\mathcal{L}_{\text{false-mot}}=\lVert(\hat{x}-x_t)\odot(1-m)\rVert_1$ penalizes
predicted change outside the region $m$ that actually moved, discouraging
background flicker; and (iv)
$\mathcal{L}_{\text{bright}}=\lvert\,\overline{\hat{x}}-\overline{x^\star}\,\rvert$
penalizes drift in mean frame intensity, the term that directly fights the
brightness blow-up we observe at rollout. The weights
$(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\lambda_3)$ are the single most consequential knobs in this
model: \S\ref{sec:det} shows that $\lambda_3$ alone selects which way the rollout
collapses. We train with AdamW and, after a short teacher-forced warmup, add a
short ($6$-step) backpropagated rollout term so the model sees its own outputs
during training---a partial, and as we will see insufficient, remedy for exposure
bias~\cite{bengio2015scheduled}.

\subsection{Discrete-token world model}
\textbf{Tokenizer.} A VQ-VAE~\cite{oord2017vqvae} encodes each $128\times128$
grayscale frame with a fully convolutional encoder to an $8\times$-downsampled
$16\times16$ grid of $D$-dimensional vectors. Each cell $z$ is quantized to its
nearest codebook entry, $q(z)=e_{k}$ with $k=\arg\min_j\lVert z-e_j\rVert$, over a
codebook of $K{=}512$ vectors, yielding $256$ discrete tokens per frame.
Gradients pass through the non-differentiable $\arg\min$ via the
straight-through estimator, and the encoder is held near the codebook by a
commitment loss $\beta\lVert z-\mathrm{sg}[e_k]\rVert^2$; rather than learning the
codebook by gradient descent we update it with exponential moving averages of the
assigned encodings~\cite{oord2017vqvae}, the standard defense against codebook
collapse. Because a uniform pixel-reconstruction loss would happily drop the
one- to two-pixel ball, we weight the reconstruction by object presence (a large
multiplier on bright foreground pixels) so the ball survives quantization
(Figure~\ref{fig:recon}). The tokenizer is trained first and then \emph{frozen};
the dynamics model never backpropagates into it.

\textbf{Dynamics.} A GPT-style causal transformer~\cite{vaswani2017attention,
chen2020igpt} models the flattened, action-interleaved token sequence
$[f_0,a_0,f_1,a_1,\dots]$, where each $f_t$ is the $256$ tokens of frame $t$
(scanned in raster order) and $a_t$ is a single action token placed immediately
\emph{before} frame $f_{t+1}$. The vocabulary is the union of $512$ frame codes
and $4$ action codes; placing the action token just before the frame it
conditions makes every next-frame prediction explicitly action-conditioned under
the causal mask. We add learned positional embeddings over the flattened
sequence and train with next-token cross-entropy, computing the loss \emph{only}
on frame-token positions---actions are given control inputs, not quantities to
predict. At generation we autoregressively sample frame tokens with temperature
$\tau$ and top-$k$ truncation, append the (known) action token, decode the
completed frame with the frozen tokenizer, and slide a fixed-length context
window so rollouts can run past the training context length. A key--value cache
avoids recomputing attention over the prefix at each step and speeds generation
by roughly an order of magnitude.

\textbf{Why tokens.} Two properties of this design target the exact mechanisms
behind the deterministic model's collapse. First, a cross-entropy objective over
discrete codes models the full conditional distribution and is \emph{not}
minimized by averaging distinct futures, so it avoids the mean-seeking blur that
an $L_1$/$L_2$ regressor suffers when several continuations are
plausible~\cite{mathieu2016deep}; sampling from that distribution further yields
distinct futures from the same prime. Second, frames are reconstructed
from \emph{codebook picks}, not from a continuously regressed intensity, so there
is no mechanism by which brightness can gradually drift up to white-out or decay
to a frozen frame---the prediction is always a decode of valid, in-distribution
codes. Discreteness thus removes both the multi-modality blur and the
intensity-drift failure at once, and we confirm in \S\ref{sec:det}--\ref{sec:tok} that the
brightness-conservation and residual-scale machinery the deterministic model
needs is unnecessary here.

\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Setup and metrics}
All models are trained with AdamW. The deterministic model ($\sim$10.4M params)
trains on a single GPU; the tokenizer ($\sim$6.2M params) and transformer
($\sim$26.8M params) train on an A100. We evaluate: (1) one-step teacher-forced
quality; (2) \emph{rollout coherence}---whether motion persists and the image
stays valid under closed-loop generation; (3) brightness stability (max pixel
over the rollout); (4) action sensitivity, $\|\hat{x}(a_i)-\hat{x}(a_j)\|$; and
(5) stochasticity, whether samples from the same prime diverge. Baselines are
copy-last-frame and ablations of the deterministic model. Model hyperparameters
are listed in Table~\ref{tab:hp}.

\subsection{The deterministic model hits a wall}
\label{sec:det}
The deterministic model learns clean one-step teacher-forced predictions, and
FiLM is necessary for it to use the action: action sensitivity rises from exactly
$0.000$ (concatenation) to $\sim$0.20 (FiLM). However, free rollout fails, and
the failure \emph{mode} is governed by the brightness-regularization weight
(Figure~\ref{fig:det}). With weak regularization the prediction's maximum pixel
value climbs from $0.58$ to $1.0$ within $\sim$6 frames and the screen whites out
(\emph{blow-up}). With strong regularization the per-frame motion collapses from
$\sim$12 to $\sim$1 and the frame freezes (\emph{freeze}). Strikingly, no
intermediate setting is stable---tuning trades one failure for the other. In earlier experiments we also
observed copy-last-frame collapse and mode collapse to a
mean ``brick-wall'' frame, all consistent with the same underlying cause: L1
regression cannot represent multi-modal futures and has no mechanism to halt
intensity drift in closed loop.

\subsection{The token model produces coherent rollouts}
\label{sec:tok}
The VQ-VAE reaches a reconstruction loss of $0.0034$ at $128\times128$ with the
ball and paddle clearly reconstructed (Figure~\ref{fig:recon}). The transformer
trains from a cross-entropy of $0.30$ down to $0.0084$. As shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:tok}, rollouts primed on four real frames stay \emph{coherent
over the full clip}: the ball moves and bounces, the paddle tracks, and bricks
persist---no blow-up and no freeze. We evaluate the token model on the
\emph{same} axes used to diagnose the deterministic failure
(Figure~\ref{fig:coh}, blue): averaged over 30 evaluation sequences, its max pixel
stays flat at $\approx$0.58 (matching ground truth, vs.\ the blow-up regime's
drift to $1.0$) and its per-step motion stays within the ground-truth band
($\approx$1$\times10^{-3}$, vs.\ the freeze regime's collapse to
$\approx$0.3$\times10^{-3}$). Unlike both deterministic regimes, which leave the
GT band within $\sim$6 steps, the token rollout never diverges over the full
clip---turning the qualitative ``coherent'' claim into a measured one.

\textbf{Codebook utilization.} Encoding 200 sequences ($1.02$M token slots), we
find only $7$ of the $512$ codes are ever used, with an empirical perplexity of
$4.9$; the top two codes cover $65\%$ of all tokens and the top five cover $95\%$
(Figure~\ref{fig:codebook}). This is \emph{expected} for low-entropy Breakout
frames rather than a sign of harmful codebook collapse: most $8\!\times\!8$
patches are pure background or uniform brick texture, so few distinct codes are
\emph{needed}. Three lines of evidence rule out a degenerate ``lookup-table''
explanation. First, reconstruction is faithful---the one- to two-pixel ball,
paddle, and bricks all survive quantization (Figure~\ref{fig:recon})---which a
handful of indistinct codes could not achieve. Second, the representation is not
$5$ codes but a $16\!\times\!16$ \emph{spatial grid} of $256$ cells each drawn
from this small alphabet, so ball position is encoded by \emph{where} the
foreground code appears, not by code identity alone. Third, the active codes are
well-separated: their embedding vectors have a mean pairwise distance of
$4.2$ (minimum $1.8$), rather than collapsed onto a
single point. The small alphabet thus reflects the data's low visual entropy, not
a degenerate encoder.

\subsection{Stochasticity}
Because generation samples from the predicted token distribution, the same prime
yields different futures. Figure~\ref{fig:stoch} shows three rollouts from
identical priming frames with different random seeds; at matched timesteps the
ball follows different trajectories, indicating the model learned a
\emph{distribution} over futures rather than a single continuation. This is the
action-conditioned stochastic simulation we set out to build, and a capability
the deterministic model cannot provide (Table~\ref{tab:cmp}).

\subsection{Ablations}
Two ablations of the deterministic model isolate our design choices.
\emph{Removing skip connections} reverts the model to a flat bottleneck and
produces blurry, fading objects, confirming skips are needed for sub-pixel
spatial detail. \emph{Replacing FiLM with concatenation} drives action
sensitivity to exactly $0.000$---the model ignores the action---whereas FiLM
yields $\sim$0.20. For the token model, the brightness-conservation term and
residual-scale tuning that were critical for the deterministic model are
\emph{unnecessary}: discreteness removes the intensity-drift failure outright.

\section{Conclusion}
We presented a controlled study of action-conditioned world modeling on Atari
Breakout. A deterministic FiLM U-Net learns clean one-step predictions but cannot
roll out stably: its failure is a regularization-controlled trade-off between
brightness blow-up and motion freeze, with no stable operating point---the
exposure-bias wall of pixel regression. A two-stage discrete-token model
(VQ-VAE + causal transformer) breaks this wall, producing coherent, stochastic, action-conditioned rollouts. We also
showed that a salient failure (ball respawn) is a data-coverage issue and fixed it
at the source. Future work includes quantifying the coherence horizon against
ground truth with per-frame error curves, temperature/top-$k$ ablations, color
(RGB) frames for visual realism, and extending to additional games. More broadly,
our results reinforce a lesson from the recent world-model literature: for stable,
diverse rollouts, the representation and objective (discrete tokens with
classification and sampling) matter more than tuning a pixel-regression loss.
